Count of Los Angeles v. Superior Court
June 2, 2012

A claim for pain and suffering under federal civil rights law does not survive the plaintiff's death.
Plaintiff Patricia Cordova sued the Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department for sexual harassment, asserting state law violations and federal civil rights violations under 42 U.S.C. 1983. She died in a car accident while the action was pending. The county claimed that her claims for pain and suffering did not survive and could not be asserted by her estate, even under her federal civil rights claims.
In California, although an estate may recover penalties, punitive or exemplary damages, an estate may not recover damages for a decedent's pain, suffering or disfigurement. Code of Civil Procedure § 377.34. It was clear that any state claims for pain and suffering were barred, but the trial court and court of appeal held that the estate's claims for Ms. Cordova's pain and suffering under her federal civil rights claim survived and could be asserted. The California Supreme Court reversed, reasoning that a federal court sitting in California and hearing a section 1983 claim would use California's law to "fill deficiencies in federal law" and would apply California's survival law banning an estate from recovering damages for the decedent's pain and suffering.
